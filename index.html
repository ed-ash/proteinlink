The Problem

Africa's protein supply chain
is fundamentally broken.

Ghana produces abundant livestock — yet modern buyers can't get consistent, safe, traceable protein at scale. The result? Waste, inflated costs, and missed opportunity.

💸

High & Volatile Input Costs

Animal prices, feed, and logistics fluctuate wildly with no coordinated procurement. Informal middlemen absorb 30–40% of farmer margins.

30–40% lost to middlemen
🏭

No Integrated Processing

Scattered third-party abattoirs operate well below food safety standards. Carcass handling, chilling, and yield management are near-absent across the sector.

Below food safety standards
🧊

Critical Cold Chain Gaps

Spoilage rates exceed 20% post-slaughter. Temperature breaks disqualify local suppliers from Shoprite, Melcom, and major institutional buyers.

>20% spoilage post-slaughter
📦

Invisible Traceability

Modern retailers demand farm-to-fork traceability. Ghana's informal sector has none — locking out billions in potential domestic sourcing contracts.

Zero traceability infrastructure
The consequence: Modern retailers like Shoprite, Melcom and Palace struggle to reliably source locally. Restaurant chains import special cuts they need for their business — protein that could be Ghanaian, buying from Ghanaians.

Our Story

Two paths.
One shared vision.

ProteinLink was born in the classroom of the maiden Ashesi MBA cohort — where Edmund and Kojo sat down and realised their two very different careers had been quietly building toward the same destination.

Kojo had spent years deep in Ghana's agricultural trenches — moving pork, catfish, and beef across Accra's busiest market channels. He'd built a 55-acre piggery with multi-year institutional relationships, proven logistics know-how, and a marketing instinct sharpened by co-running one of Ghana's leading agencies — a firm behind landmark campaigns, including Twitter's very first activation in Ghana.

Edmund brought a decade of technology and commercial strategy. He had navigated Ghana's telecom sector from scrappy startups to established operators, consulted regulatory bodies on compliance and monitoring, and helped business professionals apply data skills in their daily work through edtech. In 2024, he answered a long-held passion and began rearing livestock, planting maize and soya bean — and within four months of launching his beef venture, he secured an anchor contract supplying 3 metric tons every month to the biggest food chain in Ghana.

"We're not building this from scratch — we're merging two operating businesses with real revenue, real clients, and a combined decade of market presence. ProteinLink is the infrastructure Ghana's protein sector has always needed."
